### What we are learning in this section

In this section, we are learning about **Microsoft Secure Score**. This is your report card for your organization's security. We are going to look at how Microsoft measures how "hard" your systems are to crack and how you can use this score to figure out exactly which doors you need to lock to stop attackers in their tracks.

### What tf is this "Secure Score" thing?

Think of Secure Score as a holistic health check for your cloud environment. It gives you a number that tells you how secure your organization is right now; the higher the number, the better your security setup. It’s made up of two main pieces: the Microsoft Secure Score (for M365) and the Cloud Secure Score (which is risk-based for your cloud posture). These are part of the "Exposure Management" tools in the Defender portal, designed to help you stay ahead of threats.

### Why tf should I care about a number?

This isn't just vanity metrics. By following the recommendations in your Secure Score, you are actively protecting your organization from real-world threats. The dashboard gives you robust visualizations of trends, lets you see how you stack up against similar organizations, and even keeps track if third-party tools have already fixed a security gap for you. It’s a centralized command center to monitor the security of your identities, apps, and devices.

### Which tools does this thing actually watch?

You might think this only covers Windows, but it’s actually much broader. It gives security recommendations for a massive list of products, including Microsoft Defender for Office, Exchange Online, Microsoft Entra ID, Defender for Endpoint, Identity, Cloud Apps, Purview, Teams, and App governance. It also plays nice with third-party apps like Citrix ShareFile, Docusign, GitHub, Okta, Salesforce, ServiceNow, SharePoint Online, and Zoom.

### How tf do I actually raise my score?

You don't just sit there and hope for the number to go up. You go to the **Recommended actions** tab. This list shows you exactly where your "attack surfaces" are—basically, the places hackers are most likely to try and break in. You can search, filter, and group these actions to prioritize your work. If you have a different tool doing the job, or if you've decided a risk is acceptable, you can mark those actions as "covered by a third party" or "risk accepted" so they don't drag your score down.
